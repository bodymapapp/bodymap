# Therapist Marketing Playbook

**Last updated:** May 7, 2026
**Audience:** Solo licensed massage therapists who use MyBodyMap and want to grow their own practices.
**Purpose:** Seven concrete strategies a therapist can use to market herself to her clients, plus six additional income sources beyond per-session fees.

This playbook is content we share with therapists, not content for our own marketing team. It lives in the Founder Hub so HK can reference it during therapist conversations, but it is intended to be lifted into emails, blog posts, in-app guides, and printed handouts when therapists ask "how do I grow my practice."

The principles here are honest, slow-growth, retention-first. No funnel hacks. No paid spam. No tactics that would feel manipulative coming from a calm therapist who actually cares about her clients.

---

## How therapists actually grow

Before the seven strategies, the framing.

Most marketing advice for solo service businesses comes from internet marketers who do not understand wellness work. Their playbook (paid ads, SEO content, lead magnets, email funnels) treats every client as a number. That works for software. It does not work for massage therapy. Massage therapy is a relationship business. Relationship businesses grow through trust, time, and a small number of well-tended channels, not through volume.

The therapists who grow steadily over a decade tend to do five things: they retain the clients they have, they ask those clients to bring friends, they show up consistently in one or two channels, they build small offers that increase per-client revenue without increasing per-client effort, and they avoid the temptation to chase every new shiny tactic.

The seven strategies below are organized by leverage. The first three are the highest-leverage retention plays. The next two are word-of-mouth amplifiers. The last two are visibility tactics for filling the calendar when retention is already strong.

## Strategy 1: Make rebooking automatic, not a question

The single highest-leverage thing any therapist can do is to **never let a client leave the room without the next appointment booked**. Not "I will text you to schedule." Not "Check the website when you get a chance." Booked. On the calendar. Confirmed.

The math is brutal. If a regular client comes in monthly and you fail to rebook her once, you have lost a session. Five clients each missing one rebook a year equals five lost sessions, around five hundred dollars. Across thirty regulars, the loss is in the thousands.

How to do this without being pushy:
- At the end of every session, while she is still on the table, ask: "Same time next month, or earlier?"
- If she hesitates, suggest a specific date. "How about the second Tuesday at three?" Most people say yes when offered a specific time.
- Use MyBodyMap's reminder system to confirm the rebooked appointment 48 hours before.
- If she truly is not ready, set a follow-up reminder for two weeks out and personally text her.

Therapists who rebook in-room run 85-95% retention. Therapists who leave it to "she will reach out" run 40-60%. The difference is a single sentence at the end of every session.

## Strategy 2: The post-session text

Within twenty-four hours of the session, send a short personal note. Not a marketing message. A human one.

"Hi Sarah, hope you are feeling lighter after yesterday. The right shoulder was holding a lot. Try the stretches we talked about and let me know how the next few days go."

What this does:
- Reinforces that you noticed something specific about her body, not a generic spa visit
- Gives her something concrete to do between sessions, which keeps the work present
- Opens a door for her to reply with how she is doing, building the relationship outside the session

What this is not:
- A reminder to rebook (you already rebooked in-room per Strategy 1)
- A promotional offer
- An automated marketing email

The right tool for this is your phone, not your software. MyBodyMap can remind you to send the text, but the text itself is from you, written by you, specific to her. The moment it becomes templated or automated, it stops working.

Therapists who do post-session texts retain clients about 20% better than therapists who do not. The investment is two minutes per client per session.

## Strategy 3: Ask for the referral, specifically and at the right moment

Word of mouth is the most powerful growth channel for any massage practice. But "word of mouth" does not just happen. You have to ask, and you have to ask the right way.

The wrong way: posting on your business Instagram "Refer a friend and get 10% off your next session." This feels transactional and most clients will ignore it.

The right way: at the end of a session that went particularly well, in person, while the relationship is warm:

"You mentioned earlier that your sister has been having neck pain. I would be happy to see her if she is open to it. Want me to send you my booking link to forward?"

Notice what is happening:
- You are referencing a specific person she already mentioned
- You are framing it as you doing her a favor (helping her sister), not her doing you a favor
- You are giving her a concrete tool (the booking link) rather than vague "spread the word"

When she forwards the link, you have a warm referral arriving with both your name and her trust attached. Conversion rate from this kind of referral is around 50%, compared to 5-10% for cold inquiries.

How often to ask: with regular clients, no more than once every six months. With clients who have referred someone before, never again. They already know the model and will refer when it is right.

## Strategy 4: The thank-you note that is actually a card

Every six months, pick five regulars and send them a handwritten card. Not a postcard. A real card, in an envelope, with a stamp.

Write three sentences:
- Something specific you remember about her work, her body, or her life
- A genuine thank you for trusting you
- An invitation to come in if anything is feeling stuck

What this does:
- Reactivates dormant relationships before they go cold
- Differentiates you from every other service provider in her life (none of whom are sending handwritten cards)
- Creates a moment of genuine surprise that triggers retelling ("guess what, my massage therapist sent me a card")

The economics: thirty cards a year at five dollars each is one hundred fifty dollars. If two of those thirty cards trigger a returning client who would have lapsed, the ROI is somewhere around fifteen to one. Most therapists report better.

The trap to avoid: writing the same card to everyone. The whole point is that it is personal. If you cannot remember anything specific about her, do not send the card. Pick a different five.

## Strategy 5: The one-question email

Once every quarter, send one email to your whole client list. One question. No call to action.

Examples:
- "If you could change one thing about your last session, what would it be?"
- "What part of your body has been asking for attention lately?"
- "What would make it easier to get on my table this winter?"

Why this works:
- It positions you as someone who listens, not someone who sells
- The replies you get are gold for understanding what your clients actually want
- The act of opening an email and reading something interesting (not promotional) keeps you top-of-mind

How to do it well:
- Plain text, signed personally, sent from your real email address
- Not from a marketing platform (Mailchimp branding kills it)
- Reply personally to every response within forty-eight hours
- Never use the responses as marketing fodder ("Sarah said her shoulders hurt!" -> please do not)

## Strategy 6: One channel, done well

Most therapists try to be on Instagram, Facebook, TikTok, Yelp, Google Business, Nextdoor, and LinkedIn. They post inconsistently to all of them, get tired, and stop. The result is seven half-built channels that all look abandoned.

The better strategy: pick one channel and do it well for at least twelve months before considering a second.

Which channel to pick depends on you, not on theory:
- **If you love photography and have a beautiful studio:** Instagram. One image a week, calm aesthetic, no reels until you genuinely enjoy making them.
- **If you love writing and have a long-term body of knowledge to share:** an email newsletter through MyBodyMap. Once a month, one short essay about something you noticed across your practice.
- **If you love community and your clients are hyper-local:** Nextdoor and Facebook neighborhood groups. Authentic answers to real questions, not sales pitches.
- **If you love speaking and explaining concepts:** YouTube or a podcast. One short video a month, explaining a single concept like "what fascia actually is" or "why your right shoulder hurts."

The criterion is not "where will I get the most clients." It is "where can I sustainably show up for at least a year." Sustainability beats reach.

The one channel a therapist should always claim, even if not actively posting: Google Business Profile. It is free, it is the source of truth for hours and address, and it is where clients verify you exist. Update it, claim the photos, respond to reviews.

## Strategy 7: Workshops, classes, and in-person community

For therapists who have built a strong solo practice and want to grow without burning out, the highest-leverage move is to teach, not to take more clients.

Examples that work:
- A free monthly self-care workshop in your studio (45 minutes, 8-12 people, focused on one body region)
- A paid four-week posture clinic (four weekly sessions, $200 per attendee, 6-8 attendees)
- A guest spot at a local yoga studio teaching "what to feel in your shoulders during downward dog"
- A continuing education event at the local massage school (paid)

What teaching does:
- Positions you as the expert your community calls when something is wrong
- Generates leads passively (students become clients)
- Creates a per-attendee revenue stream that does not scale linearly with your time
- Is genuinely interesting, which is good for not burning out

What teaching is not:
- A way to "get clients" through bait-and-switch (the workshop has to be valuable on its own)
- A side hustle separate from the practice (it should reinforce the practice)
- Something you do once and abandon (it works through consistency)

## Other sources of income beyond per-session fees

Many solo therapists earn 10-30% of their income from sources besides per-session fees. Worth exploring once the core practice is steady.

### Memberships and packages
The most predictable secondary income. A monthly membership at $150 for one session per month, or a six-pack at $480 for $80 per session. Both reduce decision friction for the client and stabilize cash flow for you. MyBodyMap supports both natively.

### Gift certificates
Often dismissed as "the holiday thing" but actually a year-round revenue stream. Some clients buy one for a friend's birthday. Some new clients buy one for themselves as a "let me try this person." Margin is high (no session has been delivered yet). MyBodyMap supports gift certificates from your booking page.

### Product retail
Massage tools (rollers, balls, theracane), home oil blends, branded merchandise. Margin: 50-70% on most products if sourced wisely. Best for therapists who already have a beautiful studio space and clients who linger after the session. Inventory management is the limiter.

### Specialty modalities at premium pricing
Most therapists charge a single price for a session regardless of modality. Some specialties (manual lymphatic drainage, oncology massage, prenatal, sports rehabilitation) command 30-50% higher rates because they require advanced training. If you have the training, charge for it.

### Workshops and continuing education
Per Strategy 7. The teaching model.

### Affiliate income from products you actually use
If you genuinely recommend a specific oil brand, table, or tool, set up an affiliate link. Be honest about it. Clients respect honesty about how you make money. They do not respect hidden affiliate marketing.

### What we do not recommend as income sources
- Insurance billing (regulatory complexity, paperwork, often not worth the per-hour rate for solo practice)
- MLM products (almost always damages the practice's credibility)
- Online courses sold to other therapists (different business, different skill set, dilutes focus)
- Cryptocurrency or "investment" schemes pitched to clients (we should not have to say this, but it has come up)

## Tying it all together

The strategies above are not a checklist. A therapist who tries to do all seven plus all six income sources at once will burn out within sixty days.

The right approach: pick one or two strategies that fit your personality, do them consistently for ninety days, then evaluate. Add a third only if the first two are stable.

If forced to recommend a starting set:
1. **Strategy 1 (rebook in-room)** · high leverage, low effort, can be implemented today
2. **Strategy 2 (post-session text)** · high leverage, slight effort, builds the personal layer
3. **Strategy 3 (specific referral asks)** · medium leverage, requires comfort asking, but pays out over years

Three strategies, done well for a year, will grow most solo practices by 20-40% in revenue without adding hours of work. That is the model.
