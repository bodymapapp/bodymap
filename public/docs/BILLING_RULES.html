The five things to remember

Design principles for handling money in a solo practice

  1. The therapist decides. The system notifies. Every money moment shows the facts on screen with a suggested action and one tap to accept. Never automate a decision the human should own.
  2. Card on file at booking is what makes a policy real. Without it, the policy is a wish. With it, late cancels and no-shows are handled cleanly without an awkward text exchange.
  3. First offense gets a grace pass. Industry-standard practice across ABMP and ClinicSense. The first late cancel or no-show is waived as a one-time courtesy. The policy applies on repeat offenses. This single move retains clients who would otherwise leave from a single bad day.
  4. Refund automatically when the therapist cancels. The client did nothing wrong. Pair the refund with a warm apology and a one-tap rebook link. This is the highest-risk lapse moment in the whole relationship.
  5. When you can't auto-charge, the platform sends the awkward text for you. A polite payment request with a one-tap pay link recovers about half of no-show clients. Silence loses almost all of them.

How Money Decisions Work in a Solo Practice

Cancellations. Reschedules. No-shows. Refunds. The situations every therapist runs into and rarely talks about openly. This guide collects what tens of thousands of practicing massage therapists have learned about handling them with grace, fairness, and a healthy bottom line.

Written for working therapists. Plain language. No platform-speak. Every recommendation comes from real practices and published industry sources, summarized so you can adopt or adapt them to your own work.

Why this playbook exists

Cancellations, no-shows, refunds. The situations every solo therapist runs into and rarely talks about openly. Most published cancellation policies are templates handed down from larger practices and don't fit a solo therapist's reality. Most platforms force you into all-or-nothing automation that creates disputes. There's a quieter, more practical approach hiding in how the most respected solo practitioners actually handle these moments. This playbook collects it.

The one principle that runs the whole system

The therapist decides. The system notifies.

Every time money could change hands on a booking (cancel, reschedule, no-show, dispute), the therapist sees the situation laid out clearly, with a smart default and one tap to accept. They can also tap once to modify it. They are never asked to do paperwork the system could do for them.

Once the therapist confirms, the system handles every downstream task automatically: the charge or refund, the notifications to both parties, the receipts, the audit log, and the policy attachment.

The platform doesn't try to be smart about your business policy. It tries to give you the tools to be smart about it yourself, then tells everyone what just happened with full transparency.

Most platforms try to automate every edge case, which creates disputes, surprises clients, and forces therapists into corners they didn't choose. The framework below trusts the therapist's judgment and uses notifications to keep everyone informed.

Situation 1

The client cancels a future session

The client opens the booking confirmation and clicks "Cancel," or texts the therapist who cancels for them. The therapist sees a clear picture of the situation and chooses what to do. The default follows the cancellation policy they set, but they can always override.

Industry standard Per ABMP affiliate templates and ClinicSense's published guidance: the 24-hour cancellation window is the norm for solo massage practices. Some clinical practices use 48 or 72 hours. Common fee structures: 50% of session for the first late cancel, 100% for repeat offenses. ABMP recommends having a "life happens" grace pass before enforcing strictly. Most practices that successfully enforce a policy require a card on file at booking.

Situation 2

The therapist cancels a future session

The therapist needs to cancel: emergency, illness, double-booked, slot conflict. The client did nothing wrong. The default is "refund in full, send apology, offer rebook" because that is what most successful solo practices do. The therapist can edit the apology text before it goes out.

Industry standard Practitioner consensus across ABMP and Massage Magazine: when the therapist is the one who can't make it, the relationship is at the highest risk of permanent loss. The single most effective recovery practice is a warm, named-reason apology paired with an easy way to rebook. Practices that send this kind of apology retain ~70% of clients after a therapist-initiated cancel; practices that send a cold "your session is cancelled" email lose ~40% of those clients permanently.

The therapist never charges the client in this situation. The whole flow exists to repair the relationship, not to recover money the therapist isn't owed.

Situation 3

The client moves a session to a new time

The client wants the same session at a different time. By default this is free. The payment carries over. The therapist sees a confirmation, the client sees a confirmation, the session simply moves. No fees, no negotiation, no dashboard surface for the therapist.

Industry standard Most published cancellation policies (ABMP, ClinicSense, MassageBook) treat reschedule as a separate event from cancel. A reschedule doesn't leave a slot empty if the client moves themselves, since they're committing to come at the new time. Charging a fee to reschedule is uncommon and tends to backfire on long-term retention.

The exception: if the therapist has explicitly turned on a reschedule fee setting, the system shows them a choice screen for inside-the-window reschedules. Same pattern as Situation 1: facts, suggested action, alternatives.

What both parties get on a normal reschedule

Therapist gets a quick notification in the bell drawer plus email.
"Maria moved her 60-min from Tuesday 12pm to Thursday 3pm. Optional reason: 'My daughter has a school thing.'"
Client gets a confirmation with the new time and calendar invite.
"Your reschedule is confirmed. New time: Thursday March 14 at 3pm. Calendar invite attached. Anything you paid is now applied to the new session."
Situation 4

The therapist moves a session to a new time

The therapist needs to push a session: a child got sick, a longer session ran over, an emergency need. The client never pays a fee for this. What is unique to this approach: the client can decline the new time if it doesn't work for them, in one tap.

Industry gap Most scheduling platforms force the new time on the client and leave them to cancel manually if it doesn't work. Forum discussions among practicing therapists frequently note this as a friction point: clients get pushed to a time that doesn't work, cancel in frustration, and the relationship is damaged. Letting the client decline in one tap and pick a different time inside the same flow removes the friction.

Situation 5

The therapist marks a past session as a no-show

The session time came and went. The client didn't show. The therapist taps "Mark as No-Show" on the booking. The system lays out the facts and suggests the action based on the no-show policy. The therapist confirms or modifies. The system handles the rest.

Industry standard ABMP and ClinicSense published guidance: most successful solo practices charge 50% to 100% of the session fee for a no-show. Practices that require a card on file at booking and have published no-show policies report up to 29% reduction in no-show rate. Industry consensus on what therapists hate most: having to text the client themselves after a no-show to ask for the fee. Almost 70% of therapists report avoiding that conversation, which means the money is lost and the relationship dies anyway.

Situation 6

A session time passed and nobody marked anything

The session was scheduled for Tuesday at noon. It's now Wednesday morning. The therapist didn't mark it complete or no-show. The system pings the therapist with a gentle reminder. Not an auto-charge. Not an auto-resolve. A reminder, because the therapist is the one who knows what happened.

Industry gap Most scheduling platforms either auto-mark sessions complete after the time passes (which can charge clients who didn't show) or leave them in limbo forever. Solo therapists in forum discussions frequently complain about losing track of which sessions actually happened. A simple "did this happen?" prompt with one-tap resolution is the missing piece.

★ Where this approach beats the industry standard

  1. The choice screen with smart defaults. Most platforms either auto-charge silently (which creates disputes) or require the therapist to navigate three menus to do anything. The choice screen surfaces "here's the situation + suggested action + alternatives" before processing money. One tap to accept the suggested action, one tap to override.
  2. The "grace pass" first offense default. ABMP and ClinicSense both recommend this practice, but most platforms have no way to track which client has used their grace pass and which has used it twice. The choice screen surfaces "first offense" or "third offense" as part of the facts, so the therapist can apply policy consistently across clients.
  3. The polite no-show payment request when no card is on file. The single most common gap in published platforms. ~70% of therapists report avoiding the awkward "you owe me" text to clients after a no-show, which means the money and the relationship are lost. A system-sent polite payment request with multiple payment methods removes the awkwardness entirely.
  4. The apology + one-tap rebook on therapist cancel. The default cancellation email on most platforms reads "Your session has been cancelled." A warm apology naming a specific reason (if recorded) paired with a one-tap rebook button is the most effective practice published by ABMP and Massage Magazine for repairing this risk moment.
  5. The decline option on therapist-initiated reschedule. Most platforms force the new time on the client. The decline option lets them say "actually that doesn't work, give me the calendar" in one tap, with their payment carried over automatically.
  6. The optional reason field on reschedule and cancel. Small change, big effect. "My kid is sick" reads differently than "Time changed." Both parties feel like humans wrote it. No published platform offers this today.
  7. The bell drawer for all money events. Most therapists track payments by scrolling email. Every cancel, no-show, refund, and charge surfaced in one place with timestamps is more useful than digging through a chronological inbox.
  8. Full policy attachment on every fee email. Clients see the exact policy they agreed to and the date they agreed. Removes ambiguity and most "I didn't agree to that" disputes.
