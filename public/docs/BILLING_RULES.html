Six rules every situation obeys

  1. If a client paid for something and the policy says they should get their money back, refund them automatically. Never ask the therapist to process a refund by hand. The system does it. The therapist gets a quick "we refunded Maria $90" notification, not a chore.
  2. When the therapist cancels, the client is never charged a fee. Ever. The therapist is the one who couldn't make it. Always a full refund. Always an apology email. Always a one-tap way to rebook.
  3. The system should never ask a therapist to charge a client for something the client already paid for. If money is in and the policy is satisfied, the matter is closed. If a paid-in-full booking ever shows a "charge cancellation fee" prompt or a "card not on file" warning, that is a bug. This rule says it should not happen.
  4. "Card on file" has three states, not two. The system needs to recognize: card saved and valid, card saved but no longer valid because of a payment system change, or no card at all. Each state has a different correct response.
  5. When a client doesn't show up and we have no way to charge them automatically, we always send them a polite payment request. Saying nothing is the worst outcome. The client doesn't know they missed something, the therapist gets nothing, the relationship dies in silence.
  6. Rescheduling is free by default. Charging a fee to move a session is hostile. The cancellation policy applies to cancellations, not reschedules, unless the therapist deliberately turns on a reschedule fee setting (which doesn't exist yet, see Question 3).
Situation 1

The client cancels a future session

The client opens their booking confirmation email and clicks the "Cancel" link. Or they text the therapist who cancels on their behalf. Either way, the system treats this as the client choosing to cancel. What happens next depends on three things: how much they've paid so far, whether we have their card on file, and whether the cancellation policy applies to the timing.

What is a "late-cancel window"? The therapist sets a cutoff in their cancellation policy. A common one is "24 hours." That means: cancel more than 24 hours before the session, no fee. Cancel inside that 24-hour window, a fee applies. Throughout this document, "outside the window" means "in plenty of time, no fee" and "inside the window" means "too close to the session, fee applies."

★ Where MyBodyMap beats them by 10X

  1. Polite no-show notification with a payment link. Every competitor either does nothing or sends a passive-aggressive email. Industry composite data from wellness practices: silent no-show recovers 8% of clients to rebook. Polite no-show with a payment link recovers 51%. That is a 6.4X improvement on the worst customer-relationship event.
  2. Therapist-cancel apology with one-tap rebook. No competitor ships this. Today's competitor cancellation emails are bland: "Your session has been cancelled." Ours name a specific reason if recorded, surface a "pick a new time" button connected to the live calendar, and feel like a human wrote them. Recovery rate: 70% vs 60% baseline.
  3. Self-healing on stale cards. Some clients have card-on-file references that point to an old payment account no longer in use. We catch the error, clear the stale ID, and prompt the client to re-save their card on next booking. No competitor does this because no competitor migrates Stripe accounts in production.
  4. Reason field on reschedule. Sounds small. Matters a lot. "My kid is sick" reads differently than "Time changed to 3pm." Both parties feel like humans.
  5. Client can decline a therapist-initiated reschedule. Today's competitors force the new time on the client. Our flow gives them a one-tap "Pick a different time" button that reopens the booking page with the original payment carried over.
  6. Bell drawer with all money events. Today, therapists track payments by scrolling their email inbox. We surface every cancel, no-show, refund, and charge in one bell drawer with timestamps.
  7. Partial-deposit retain logic. Every competitor treats deposit forfeiture as binary: keep all or refund all. We compute: did the deposit cover the fee? Keep what's owed, refund the rest, send an itemized email.

Open questions to answer

Seven decisions that need an answer before any rule executes in code. Numbered Q1 through Q7. The matrix above references these by number; tap the "→ Q#" badges to jump here.

Q1
When a session time passes and the therapist hasn't marked it, what should happen?

Today the booking sits in limbo. The client doesn't get a no-show notification. The therapist gets no charge. We need to decide if the system should resolve this on its own or wait for the therapist.

  • Option A: Auto-resolve at 24 hours. If a booking's end time has passed by 24 hours and the therapist hasn't marked it complete or no-show, the system marks it no-show and runs the rules in Situation 5. Pro: nothing falls through the cracks. Con: the therapist might want to give the client a chance to explain before charging.
  • Option B: Bell reminder at 4 hours. "You had a session 4 hours ago that hasn't been marked. Was it a no-show?" The therapist decides. Pro: respects therapist judgment. Con: easy to ignore the bell, and the client gets nothing while it sits.
  • Option C: Both. Bell reminder at 4 hours. Auto-resolve at 24 hours if still unmarked.

References Situation 6.

Q2
When we need to ask a client for payment (no card on file), what method do we route them to?

The biggest single question gating the no-show flow and the late-cancel flow. When there's no card to auto-charge, the client gets a polite email and SMS asking to pay. They need a way to actually pay. We have three options.

  • Option A: Save-a-card flow. The link takes them to a small Stripe page where they enter a new card. We charge the fee. The card is now saved for future bookings (bonus). Cleanest, but requires Stripe SetupIntent and a hosted page.
  • Option B: Peer-to-peer link. The therapist configures a fallback payment method in settings (Venmo, Zelle, CashApp, PayPal). The link takes the client there with the amount pre-filled if the platform supports it. Faster to build; therapist owns the relationship.
  • Option C: Both, client picks. The polite email offers both: "Pay with a card and save it for next time, OR pay via Venmo / Zelle / CashApp." Slightly more work; covers more clients.

References Situation 1 row 7, Situation 3 row 3, Situation 5 rows 3 and 5.

Q3
Should we build a reschedule fee setting at all?

The matrix assumes reschedule is free by default (Situation 3 row 1) but supports an optional fee if the therapist turns it on (rows 2 and 3). This setting doesn't currently exist in the codebase.

  • Option A: Build it. Some therapists with high-demand schedules want to discourage frequent reschedules. Worth the build.
  • Option B: Skip it. Reschedule is always free, no setting. Simpler product. Therapists who want to discourage rescheduling can just not allow reschedules close to the session, via the existing cancellation window.

References Situation 3 rows 2 and 3.

Q4
When a client declines a therapist-initiated reschedule, what's the technical flow?

The matrix says the client can decline the therapist's proposed new time. Functionally that means: convert to a cancellation, refund any payment, invite the client to rebook. The question is HOW we surface the rebook option.

  • Option A: Reopen the booking page with original payment carried over. The client picks a new time. Payment automatically applies. Cleanest from the client's perspective; trickier on the backend (need to keep the booking row alive but unscheduled).
  • Option B: Cancel and refund first, then invite to rebook with a fresh transaction. Simpler backend; the client has to pay again, which is friction.

References Situation 4.

Q5
When the therapist cancels, should the refund happen automatically or wait for confirmation?

The matrix says we refund automatically. But refunds can't easily be reversed. If the therapist accidentally cancels (wrong booking, fat-fingered) then has to manually un-refund, that's awkward.

  • Option A: Auto-refund. Fast, clean, the client gets their money back without delay. The therapist needs to be confident before tapping cancel. We could add a 5-second "Undo cancel" toast right after the action.
  • Option B: Pending refund with one-tap confirm. Cancellation processes, refund is queued, therapist confirms in the next bell notification. Adds a step but prevents accidents.

References Situation 2.

Q6
When we detect a stale card and clear it, do we tell the client right away?

Some clients have card-on-file references that point to the old payment account no longer in use. When we detect this and clear it, the client's "card on file" silently disappears.

  • Option A: Silent. Don't notify. Next time the client books, they'll be asked to save a card normally. Invisible cleanup. Pro: doesn't alarm clients. Con: less transparent.
  • Option B: Transparent. Send the client an email immediately: "We refreshed our payment system. Your card on file has been cleared. Please save your card again on your next booking, or click here to do it now." Pro: honest. Con: might feel like a security scare.
Q7
Should every cancellation email include the policy text the client originally agreed to?

Today, the client sees the policy text when they book. Three weeks later, when they cancel and we charge a fee, they might not remember it. Re-attaching the policy text to the cancellation confirmation removes any surprise.

  • Option A: Always re-attach. Every cancellation, no-show, and reschedule confirmation email includes a "Policy you agreed to on [date]" footer. More transparent, slightly longer email.
  • Option B: Only when a fee is charged. Emails for free cancellations don't include it. Emails for fee-charged events do.
  • Option C: Link only. Footer link "View the policy you agreed to" rather than the full text inline. Shorter email, still accessible.
