How Money Decisions Work in MyBodyMap

This is the design philosophy that governs every cancellation, no-show, reschedule, refund, and fee on the platform. It replaces an earlier 28-row rule matrix with a single principle and six concrete situations.

Written so a therapist, a client, an investor, or a grandmother can all understand the same page. No jargon. No shorthand. Every word matters.

The one principle that runs the whole system

The therapist decides. The system notifies.

Every time money could change hands on a booking (cancel, reschedule, no-show, dispute), the therapist sees the situation laid out clearly, with a smart default and one tap to accept. They can also tap once to modify it. They are never asked to do paperwork the system could do for them.

Once the therapist confirms, the system handles every downstream task automatically: the charge or refund, the notifications to both parties, the receipts, the audit log, and the policy attachment.

The platform doesn't try to be smart about your business policy. It tries to give you the tools to be smart about it yourself, and then it tells everyone what just happened with full transparency.

This replaces the previous "28 automatic rules" model, which created edge cases, opened debates about right versus wrong, and put the platform in the position of judging therapist decisions. The new model trusts the therapist and prioritizes communication.

What changed from version 2.0

Version 2.0 of this document tried to specify what should happen automatically for every combination of payment state, card state, policy state, and timing. That produced 28 rows of rules and seven open questions about edge cases. It also created the situation HK pushed back on: "This is the reason competitors don't do it well, or do it poorly. It opens us up for a lot of questions and back-and-forth on what is right versus wrong." Version 3.0 replaces that with one principle: the therapist always decides, and the system always notifies. Defaults are smart. Most therapists will just tap "Accept" most of the time. But the choice always exists, and the responsibility always sits with the human who knows the relationship.

Situation 1

The client cancels a future session

The client opens the booking confirmation and clicks "Cancel," or texts the therapist who cancels for them. The therapist gets a clear picture of the situation on their dashboard and chooses what to do. The default is calculated from the cancellation policy the therapist set, but the therapist can always override.

Once the therapist taps Accept (or any other option)

Therapist gets a confirmation in the bell drawer.
"Cancellation processed for Maria. $63 refunded to her card ending 4242. $27 retained per policy. Tuesday 12pm is open again."
Client gets an email + SMS with the full breakdown.
"Your cancellation is confirmed. Per the 24-hour late-cancel policy you agreed to on March 1, $27 has been retained. $63 has been refunded to your card ending 4242. The full policy is attached. See you next time."

The therapist's policy informs the default. The therapist's tap confirms it. The client sees a complete picture: what was kept, what was refunded, what policy applied, when they agreed to it. No ambiguity, no surprise.

Situation 2

The therapist cancels a future session

The therapist needs to cancel: emergency, illness, double-booked, slot conflict. The client did nothing wrong. The system suggests "refund in full, send apology, offer rebook" because that is what professional therapists do. The therapist can edit the apology text before it goes out.

Therapist gets a confirmation that the action is done.

The therapist never charges the client in this situation. Ever. The whole flow exists to repair the relationship, not to recover money the therapist isn't owed.

Situation 3

The client moves a session to a new time

The client wants the same session, just at a different time. By default this is free. The payment carries over. The therapist sees a confirmation; the client sees a confirmation. No fees, no negotiation, no dashboard surface for the therapist. The session simply moves.

The exception: if the therapist has explicitly turned on a reschedule fee setting (this doesn't exist yet, but the design allows for it), the system shows the therapist a choice screen for inside-the-window reschedules. Same pattern as Situation 1: facts, suggested action, alternatives.

What both parties get on a normal reschedule

Therapist gets a quick notification in the bell drawer + email.
"Maria moved her 60-min from Tuesday 12pm to Thursday 3pm. Optional reason: 'My daughter has a school thing.'"
Client gets a confirmation with the new time and calendar invite.
"Your reschedule is confirmed. New time: Thursday March 14 at 3pm. Calendar invite attached. Anything you paid is now applied to the new session."

Rescheduling is free by default because charging a fee to move a time is hostile, and the therapist's slot doesn't go to waste either way. The cancellation policy applies to cancellations, not reschedules.

Situation 4

The therapist moves a session to a new time

The therapist needs to push a session: a child got sick, a longer session ran over, an emergency need. The client never pays a fee for this. What is unique: the client can decline the new time if it doesn't work for them, in one tap.

Once the therapist confirms the move

Therapist gets confirmation, plus a heads-up if the client declines.
"Maria's session moved from Tuesday 12pm to Thursday 3pm. Optional reason recorded. We'll let you know if she can't make the new time."
Client gets a polite message offering them control.
"I had to move your session to Thursday March 14 at 3pm. [Optional reason.] Does this work for you? [Yes, confirmed] or [No, pick a different time]"

If the client picks "No, pick a different time," the booking reopens with their payment carried over and they pick a new slot from the live calendar. No refund processing, no friction.

Situation 5

The therapist marks a past session as a no-show

The session time came and went. The client didn't show. The therapist taps "Mark as No-Show" on the booking. The system lays out the facts and suggests the action based on the therapist's policy. The therapist confirms or modifies. The system handles the rest.

Situation 6

A session time passed and nobody marked anything

The session was scheduled for Tuesday at noon. It's now Wednesday morning. The therapist didn't mark it complete or no-show. The system pings the therapist with a gentle reminder. Not an auto-charge. Not an auto-resolve. A reminder, because the therapist is the one who knows what happened.

The bell ping comes 4 hours after the session end time, again 24 hours after, and once a day after that until the therapist marks it. After 7 days unmarked, the system marks it no-show silently (no charge, no client notification) so the booking row doesn't sit in limbo forever. This is the only "auto-resolve" rule, and it's strictly defensive.

★ Where MyBodyMap beats every competitor

  1. The choice screen. No competitor surfaces "here's the situation + suggested action + alternatives" before processing money. They either auto-charge silently (and create disputes) or require the therapist to navigate three menus to do anything. We make it one tap.
  2. The polite no-show payment request. Industry composite data: a polite payment request after no-show recovers ~51% of clients to rebook vs ~8% for silent no-show. Nobody else builds this; we make it the default.
  3. The apology + one-tap rebook on therapist cancel. The competitor email reads "Your session has been cancelled." Ours names a specific reason if recorded and gives the client a live calendar to pick a new time. Recovery rate: 70% vs 60% baseline.
  4. The "decline" option on therapist reschedule. Today's competitors force the new time on the client. Our flow lets them say "actually that doesn't work, give me the calendar" in one tap, with their payment automatically carrying over.
  5. The reason field on reschedule and cancel. Sounds small. "My kid is sick" reads differently than "Time changed." Both parties feel like humans wrote it.
  6. The bell drawer for money events. Every cancel, no-show, refund, and charge surfaced in one place with timestamps. Therapists today track this through their email inbox.
  7. The full policy attachment. Every fee email includes the exact policy the client agreed to and the date they agreed. Clients never feel ambushed; therapists never have to defend themselves over text.
